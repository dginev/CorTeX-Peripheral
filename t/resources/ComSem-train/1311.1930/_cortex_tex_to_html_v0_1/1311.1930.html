Abstract

In this paper we present new results obtained during the SUMMER@ICERM 2013 program regarding the periodicity of outer billiards in the hyperbolic plane around polygonal tables which are tiles in two-piece tilings of the hyperbolic plane.

1 Introduction

Outer billiards is a simple dynamical system that was introduced by B. H. Neumann in 1950s in [9]. In 1970s, J. Moser popularized outer billiards as a toy model for planetary motion as a means of finding possible unbounded orbits [7, 8]. Since then, many mathematicians asked and answered questions about outer billiards systems in various geometries. For example, in 2004 C. Culter proved the existence of periodic orbits for polygonal tables in the Euclidean Plane (the proof is presented by S. Tabachnikov in [12]). R. Schwarz answered, in the affirmative, Moser’s question about the existence of unbounded orbits for certain polygons in [10, 11].

The main motivation for this paper is a result of Vivaldi and Shaidenko [8] that in the Euclidean case, outer billiards associated to quasi-rational polygons have all orbits bounded, see also [6, 5]. As a consequence, all orbits about a lattice polygon in the Euclidean plane are periodic. We continue the work of Dogru and Tabachnikov in [1] who studied the relationship between one-tile regular tilings of the hyperbolic plane and the outer billiards system.

For a detailed account of hyperbolic geometry and the hyperbolic plane, we direct the reader to [4], and for a survey of outer billiards, see [2, 13].

2 Definitions

The outer billiard map associated to a convex polygonal table PPP in the hyperbolic plane is defined as follows. For a point x∈ℍ2∖Pxsuperscriptℍ2Px\in\mathbb{H}^{2}\setminus P, there are two lines that pass through xxx and are tangent to the table PPP. By convention, we consider the tangent line for which PPP is on the left, from the point of view of xxx. Then we reflect xxx about the tangency (support) point to get T⁢(x)TxT(x) (See Figure 1). The map is well-defined whenever the tangency point is unique and so we are able to define the map TTT on the entire hyperbolic plane except for the clockwise continuations of the sides of PPP (see Figure 1) and their preimages under TTT. An immediate consequence of the definition is that TTT is a piecewise isometry.

Likewise, the inverse map T-1superscriptT1T^{-1} is not defined on the counterclockwise continuations of the sides of PPP. We define the web associated to PPP to be the union of all preimages under TTT of the clockwise continuation of the sides and of all preimages under T-1superscriptT1T^{-1} of the counterclockwise continuation of the sides. For each connected component of the complement of the web, the restriction of the map TnsuperscriptTnT^{n} to that component is defined by a single isometry of the hyperbolic plane for every n∈ℤnℤn\in{\mathbb{Z}}. That means that each connected component of the complement of the web maps as a whole under the iterations of TTT.

Another feature of the billiards map TTT is that it extends continuously to a continuous circle map t:S1→S1normal-:tnormal-→superscriptS1superscriptS1t:S^{1}\to S^{1} at infinity. The map ttt is defined using the same reflecting procedure. In this case the uniqueness of the support point is not needed, since the distance is infinite no matter the choice and hence the map ttt is well-defined for every point at infinity. Since ttt is a circle map, it has a well defined Poincaré rotation number ρ⁢(t)ρt\rho(t), and we will prove in section 3 that ρ⁢(t)ρt\rho(t) encodes information about the combinatorial dynamics of the outer billiards.

3 Outer Billiards on Tilings

We are studying the hyperbolic outer billiards map associated with a polygon that forms part of a two piece regular tiling of the hyperbolic plane. These tilings use two polygonal pieces, a regular MMM-gon and a regular NNN-gon that meet four in each vertex generating four right angles (See Figure 2). We describe the combinatorial dynamics for outer billiards around one of the MMM-gons. We note that the web associated to such a map will fall exactly on the grid lines of the tiling. This is because the reflection around a vertex of the table tile is just a rotation by 180∘superscript180180^{\circ} around vertices in the tiling. It follows that each tile maps as a whole under iterations of TTT.

3.1 Previous Results

Previous results describing outer billiards of tiles in the hyperbolic plane are obtained in [1]. In this paper, the authors have proved that every orbit of the outer billiard map around a right-angled regular nnn-gon is periodic. Any right-angled regular nnn-gon generates a tiling of the hyperbolic plane entirely consisting of nnn-gons. The theorems proven in the next sections have the same flavor as Theorem 4 in the above mentioned paper.

3.2 New Results

Our results extend Theorem 1 to two-piece regular tilings of the hyperbolic plane. We will denote a tiling of regular MMM-gons and regular NNN-gons as an (M,N)MN(M,N)-tiling, and we will always consider the table to be an MMM-gon. Such an (M,N)MN(M,N)-tiling exists if 1M+1N<121M1N12\frac{1}{M}+\frac{1}{N}<\frac{1}{2}. As mentioned earlier, these tilings have four shapes meeting at each vertex, two MMM-gons and two NNN-gons.

Claim 1.

Every orbit of TTT is periodic.

Proof.

The proof of this result is written in much detail in [1]. We will present here a sketch of it and will refer the reader to [1] for detailed explanations. The result is a consequence of the following lemma:

Lemma 1.

The rank of a tile is preserved under TTT.

Proof of lemma. The proof is by induction on the rank, based on geometrical observations. Observe that rank 1 tiles are preserved by TTT and notice that every rank kkk tile is adjacent to a rank k-1k1k-1 tile, where these two tiles map together under a single application of TTT. These two facts complete the base case and the step of the induction, proving the lemma.

From Lemma 1, since there are finitely many tiles of rank kkk, every tile must eventually map back to itself after mmm-iterations, for some natural number mmm. Hence the mmm-th iteration of TTT maps the entire tile to itself. This implies that T∘msuperscriptTabsentmT^{\circ m} is a rotation by either 2⁢π⁢jN2πjN\frac{2\pi j}{N} (for NNN-gons) or 2⁢π⁢j32πj3\frac{2\pi j}{3}(for triangles) around some point inside the tile. Hence T∘N⁢msuperscriptTabsentNmT^{\circ Nm} restricted to that tile is the identity if the tile is an NNN-gon and T∘3⁢msuperscriptTabsent3mT^{\circ 3m} restricted to that tile is the identity if the tile is a triangle. We conclude that every orbit of TTT is periodic. ∎

We will refer from now on to this self-similar structure as the crochet pattern. To describe the crochet pattern, we consider NNN-gons to be of two types (See figure 4), XXX-type and YYY-type. Type XXX NNN-gons have two “parents” in the sense that they touch two NNN-gons of the previous rank, while type YYY NNN-gons touch only one “parent”. The rank 1 NNN-gons are of neither of the types, having 0 parents, so we call them type ‘0’ NNN-gons. (This is why our counting argument begins with counting rank 222 NNN-gons.)

The methods used to prove this claim have been developed by Poincaré and we will not dwell on the details here. The reader can find extensive explanation in The Symmetry of Things [3].

In the local picture, the difference between a type XXX 777-gon and a type YYY 777-gon is encoded in the different types of degenerated heptagons we associate to them. Now by reducing the triangles in the global picture to points, we notice that the heptagons must meet 3 in each vertex. This results in the crochet pattern shown in Figure 4 (left). This crochet pattern immediately implies the claimed replacement rules. ∎

Remark 1.

(i) One might expect the formulas in Theorem 2 to also work for N=6N6N=6, i.e., a (3,6)36(3,6)-tiling of the Euclidean plain. That is not the case even though the crochet pattern works exactly the same also in the (3,6)36(3,6)-tiling. The difference that appears when computing the formulas in the (3,6)36(3,6)-tiling is that the matrix of the difference system is not diagonalizable anymore and so its powers look completely different.

(ii) Note that the determinant of all the matrices given by the crochet pattern is 1. We believe this is true because the crochet pattern replacement can also be reversed, i.e., starting with rank kkk layer, we can construct the rank k-1k1k-1 layer.

3.2.2 General (M,N)MN(M,N)-tilings

The theorem and subsequent proof are analogous to those in the (3,N)3N(3,N) case in the previous section, but we must consider the cases separately due to a difference in the counting method. In the previous section, NNN-gons were classified into types XXX and YYY, having two parents and one parent, respectively. However, due to the difference in geometry of triangles versus generic MMM-gons, the tilings in the M≥4M4M\geq 4 case never produce NNN-gons with two parents. In this case, NNN-gons either have one parent or no parent, which we denote as types YYY and ZZZ.
